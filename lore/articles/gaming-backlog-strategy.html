Your Gaming Backlog Strategy is Wrong — Here's a Better One

📅 Jun 22, 2026 ⏱ 5 min read By Heezo

You have 200+ games in your library. You've finished maybe 30 of them. Every Steam sale adds five more. Every Game Pass update adds ten. You open your library, stare at it for fifteen minutes, feel overwhelmed, and boot up the same comfort game you've played 400 hours of. Sound familiar?

Your backlog isn't the problem. Your approach to it is.

Most people treat their backlog like a to-do list. Oldest first. Alphabetical. By platform. By genre. All of these are wrong because they ignore the one variable that actually determines whether you'll finish a game: your current energy level and mood.

The Rotation Method

Here's the system that actually works. I've been using it for two years and I went from finishing 4 games a year to finishing 20+. It's stupid simple:

At any given time, you have exactly three active games:

  1. The Main Course — Your "big" game. 30-80 hours. RPG, open world, story-heavy. This is what you play when you have 2+ hours and mental energy. One at a time. No exceptions.
  2. The Side Dish — A shorter, lighter game. 5-15 hours. Linear action game, indie, puzzle, walking sim. This is what you play when you want to game but don't have the bandwidth for your Main Course. Also functions as a palate cleanser between big games.
  3. The Snack — A roguelike, multiplayer game, or endless game you can pick up for 20 minutes. Hades. Balatro. Tetris. Dead Cells. This is what you play when you have limited time or just want to turn your brain off.

When you finish a game in any slot, you replace it with the next game from that slot's queue. That's it. That's the whole system.

Why This Works (When Everything Else Doesn't)

The reason you don't finish games isn't because you have too many. It's because you're playing the wrong game for your current state. You come home exhausted from work and try to engage with a 60-hour JRPG with complex systems and dense narrative. Of course you bounce off it. That's not the game's fault — it's a mismatch.

The rotation method means you always have something appropriate for your energy level. Tired? Play your Snack. Got a weekend afternoon? Dive into your Main Course. Have an hour before dinner? Your Side Dish is perfect.

You don't abandon games because they're bad. You abandon games because you started them at the wrong time. The rotation prevents this entirely.

How to Build Your Queue

Pull up your full backlog. Now sort every game into one of the three categories:

  • Main Course candidates: Baldur's Gate 3, Elden Ring, Persona 5, Final Fantasy XVI, Starfield, Cyberpunk — anything 25+ hours with heavy investment
  • Side Dish candidates: Hi-Fi Rush, Celeste, What Remains of Edith Finch, Neon White, A Short Hike, Sifu — tight, completable, 5-15 hours
  • Snack candidates: Hades II, Balatro, Vampire Survivors, Slay the Spire, any competitive multiplayer — infinite, session-based, no commitment

Now pick your top 3-5 from each category. Those are your "next up" queue. Ignore everything else. It doesn't exist until a slot opens.

The Rules That Make It Work

  1. Never have two Main Courses active. You will finish neither. One at a time.
  2. The 3-hour rule: If you've played something for 3 hours and feel nothing — not curious, not engaged, not having fun — drop it. Life's too short. Move to the next in queue. No guilt.
  3. Don't count hours. "How Long to Beat" is a reference, not a deadline. Some people finish a "40 hour" game in 25. Some take 60. Who cares. Play until it's done or until the 3-hour rule kicks in.
  4. New releases go in the queue, not the active slot. Unless it's genuinely time-sensitive (spoiler-heavy multiplayer, a FromSoft launch where community discovery is part of the experience), new games wait their turn.
  5. Your Snack slot can change freely. This is the pressure valve. Swap it whenever. No commitment required.

The Mental Shift That Changes Everything

Here's the actual secret: your backlog is not a list of obligations. It's a menu. You don't feel guilty about a restaurant menu having items you haven't ordered. You pick what sounds good right now and enjoy it.

The games in your library aren't going anywhere. They don't expire. They don't judge you. Half of them cost $3 on sale and you bought them because "maybe someday." Someday is fine. Someday might be never. That's also fine.

The rotation method works because it removes decision paralysis (you always know what to play), matches energy to engagement (right game at the right time), and creates momentum (finishing a Side Dish gives you a dopamine hit that motivates tackling your Main Course).

My Current Rotation (For Reference)

  • Main Course: Metaphor: ReFantazio — 80 hours in, absolutely obsessed
  • Side Dish: Nine Sols — tight metroidvania, hitting that Sekiro itch
  • Snack: Balatro — I have a problem and I don't want to solve it

Try it for one month. Three slots. One rule (the 3-hour drop). Watch what happens to your completion rate. Then come back and tell me I was right.

Your backlog just became a menu. Now go eat.
